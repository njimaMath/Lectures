Course Overview

These lecture notes cover the fundamental aspects of multivariable differential calculus, providing a solid foundation for advanced engineering applications:

  • We defined partial derivatives as the rate of change of a multivariable function with respect to one input variable at a time, extending the familiar concept of derivatives to functions of multiple variables.
  • We studied planes and surfaces, learning how planes can be seen as graphs of linear functions and how more general surfaces can be understood via level sets and cross-sections. These geometric interpretations help visualize abstract mathematical concepts.
  • We introduced tangent planes to surfaces z=f(x,y), deriving their equation using the partial derivatives. The tangent plane provides the best linear approximation to the surface at a point, similar to how tangent lines approximate curves.
  • The concept of total derivative (differentials) was discussed, combining partial derivatives to approximate changes in the function due to multivariate inputs. This allows us to predict how small changes in inputs affect the output.
  • Using the Chain Rule, we can differentiate composite functions systematically in multiple dimensions, carefully summing contributions from each path of dependence. This powerful tool enables analysis of complex relationships between variables.
  • We saw how to perform differentiation in alternative coordinate systems like polar coordinates, applying the chain rule to relate derivatives in polar to those in Cartesian coordinates, which is especially useful for radially symmetric functions and problems with circular symmetry.
  • We defined what it means for a point to be a local extremum (maximum or minimum) of a function of several variables. We saw examples of maxima, minima, and saddle points, and emphasized that at interior extrema (assuming differentiability) the gradient must be zero (critical point).
  • Focusing on quadratic functions, we learned how to find their critical points and classify them by examining the quadratic form. This led to understanding how the second partial derivatives determine the nature of a critical point.
  • Through Taylor expansion around a critical point, we justified the second derivative test. We provided criteria involving the Hessian determinant D and the second partials to classify a critical point as a local min, local max, or saddle.
  • We discussed Jacobian matrices as a way to collect partial derivatives of vector-valued functions. The Jacobian gives a linear approximation of a multivariable mapping and its determinant indicates local scaling (particularly important in coordinate transformations and the inverse function theorem).
  • The Inverse Function Theorem was stated in essence: a non-zero Jacobian determinant ensures a local inverse exists and how to get its derivative. This result is fundamental to many advanced topics in engineering and mathematics.
  • We covered implicit differentiation and the Implicit Function Theorem, showing how to find derivatives of a function defined implicitly by an equation (like F(x,y)=0) by using dy/dx = -Fx/Fy, providing yet another powerful technique for analyzing complex relationships.
Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

  • Calculate and interpret partial derivatives of multivariable functions

  • Apply the chain rule to complex compositions of functions
  • Identify and classify critical points using the second derivative test

Section 1: Functions and Their Properties

Let $f: A \rightarrow B$ be a function where $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ and $B \subseteq \mathbb{R}$.

Basic Properties

If $f: A \rightarrow B$ and $g: A \rightarrow B$ are functions, then:

  1. $(f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)$
  2. $(f \cdot g)(x) = f(x) \cdot g(x)$
  3. $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$

For any $x \in A$ where these operations are defined.

Examples:

  1. $f + g: A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$
  2. $(f - g): A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$
  3. $f \circ g: A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, provided $g(A) \subseteq \text{Domain}(f)$

Section 2: Derivatives of Single-Variable Functions

Let $f: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be a function. The derivative of $f$ at a point $a$ is defined as:

$$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}$$

provided this limit exists.

Examples:

  1. If $f(x) = x^n$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$, then $f'(x) = nx^{n-1}$
  2. If $f(x) = \sin x$, then $f'(x) = \cos x$

Properties of Derivatives:

  1. $(f + g)'(x) = f'(x) + g'(x)$
  2. $(f \cdot g)'(x) = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)$ (Product Rule)
  3. $(f \circ g)'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$ (Chain Rule)

Section 3: Functions of Multiple Variables

Let $f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be a function.

For example, if $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, then $f$ assigns a real number to each point $(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$.

Examples:

  1. $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$ (paraboloid)
  2. $f(x, y) = \sin(xy) + y$ (a more complex surface)

Section 4: Partial Derivatives

For a function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to the $i$-th variable at a point $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ is defined as:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{x}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1}, x_i + h, x_{i+1}, \ldots, x_n) - f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)}{h}$$

provided this limit exists.

For a function $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, we have two partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x, y) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h, y) - f(x, y)}{h}$$
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x, y) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x, y+h) - f(x, y)}{h}$$

These are often denoted as $f_x(x, y)$ and $f_y(x, y)$, respectively.

Class 2: Planes and Surfaces

In three-dimensional space, the graph of a function z=f(x,y) is a surface. Simple examples of surfaces are planes given by linear equations. A general plane has equation Ax + By + Cz + D = 0, where (A,B,C) is a normal vector perpendicular to the plane. If C\neq 0, we can solve for z to write the plane as z = mx + ny + c, which is the graph of a linear function of x,y. For instance, z = 2x - 3y + 5 is a plane with slope 2 in the x-direction and slope -3 in the y-direction.

More generally, any equation F(x,y,z) = 0 defines a surface in \mathbb{R}^3. If it can be solved for z, we get z = f(x,y) as an explicit surface. For example, x^2 + y^2 - z^2 = 1 defines a two-sheeted hyperboloid surface. When z is given explicitly as f(x,y), we can visualize the surface as a deformable sheet; for each (x,y) in the domain, the surface has a height z above the (x,y)-plane.

A level curve (or contour) of a surface z=f(x,y) is the set of points (x,y) in the xy-plane where f(x,y) is a constant value. Drawing level curves in the xy-plane helps visualize the surface. For example, the level curves of z=x^2+y^2 are circles x^2+y^2=\text{constant}, which correspond to horizontal slices of the parabolic bowl shape (see Figure 1).

Figure 1: Paraboloid Level Curves - Description of Figure from LaTeX

We can similarly talk about curves in space: setting x, y, and z in terms of a parameter t yields a parametric space curve (x(t),y(t),z(t)). For example, x=\cos t,\;y=\sin t,\;z=3 (with t\in[0,2\pi]) is a horizontal circle of radius 1 at height z=3.

Understanding planes and surfaces is crucial before moving to derivatives, because derivatives (slopes) are defined on surfaces analogously to how slopes of lines are defined on curves.

Class 3: Tangent Planes

Just as a differentiable curve y=f(x) has a tangent line at a point, a differentiable surface z=f(x,y) has a tangent plane at a given point. Geometrically, the tangent plane "just touches" the surface at that point and has the same slope as the surface in every direction. The tangent plane is the planar surface that best approximates z=f(x,y) near that point.3 This is a footnote describing the approximation property of the tangent plane.

To derive the equation of the tangent plane, consider a point P=(a,b) in the domain and the corresponding point on the surface Q=(a,b,f(a,b)). If f has partial derivatives f_x and f_y at (a,b), then moving a small amount \Delta x in the x-direction changes z by approximately f_x(a,b)\,\Delta x, and moving \Delta y in the y-direction changes z by about f_y(a,b)\,\Delta y. By superposition (linearity), a small move from (a,b) to (a+\Delta x,\;b+\Delta y) produces a change

\Delta z \approx f_x(a,b)\,\Delta x + f_y(a,b)\,\Delta y

So the linear approximation of f near (a,b) is

L(x,y) = f(a,b) + f_x(a,b)\,(x-a) + f_y(a,b)\,(y-b)

This linear function L(x,y) is the equation of the tangent plane to z=f(x,y) at the point (a,b,f(a,b)). In standard form, the tangent plane equation is

z - f(a,b) = f_x(a,b)\,(x - a) + f_y(a,b)\,(y - b)

In summary, the tangent plane at (a,b,f(a,b)) has coefficients given by the partial derivatives at that point. The plane contains two special lines: one going in the x-direction with slope f_x(a,b) (keeping y=b constant), and one in the y-direction with slope f_y(a,b) (keeping x=a constant). These lines are the tangents to the surface's cross-sectional curves, and together they define the plane.

Figure 2: Tangent plane approximation at a point on a surface. The partial derivatives f_x and f_y give the slopes of the tangent plane in the x and y directions. All lines on the surface through that point lie (to first order) in this tangent plane.

Example 3.1.

Find the tangent plane to z = x^2 + y^2 at the point (1,2,5).

Solution: Here f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2. First compute the partial derivatives: f_x(x,y) = 2x and f_y(x,y) = 2y. At (a,b)=(1,2), we have f_x(1,2)=2 and f_y(1,2)=4. The surface passes through (1,2,f(1,2)) = (1,2,1^2+2^2) = (1,2,5) as given. Thus the tangent plane equation is

z - 5 = 2\,(x-1) + 4\,(y-2)

We can rewrite this as z = 2x + 4y - 3. This plane just touches the paraboloid z=x^2+y^2 at the point (1,2,5) and has the same slopes in x and y directions as the paraboloid does at that point.

Class 4: Partial and Total Derivatives

The partial derivatives f_x and f_y capture how f changes when moving purely in the x or y direction. However, if both x and y change simultaneously, the overall change in f is described by the total derivative. For a differentiable function z=f(x,y), the total differential dz combines the effects of changes in both variables:

dz = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y)\,dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x,y)\,dy = f_x(x,y)\,dx + f_y(x,y)\,dy

This formula originates from the linear approximation (tangent plane) derived above. It indicates that for small changes dx, dy, we have

\Delta z \approx f_x(x,y)\,\Delta x + f_y(x,y)\,\Delta y

In interpretation, dz is the height change one would predict by "following the tangent plane." If (dx,dy) is a small step in the xy-plane, then dz is the corresponding change in z along the tangent plane. The total derivative at (a,b) can be understood as the linear map (dx,dy) \mapsto f_x(a,b)\,dx + f_y(a,b)\,dy. In vector form, if \mathbf{u} = \langle dx, dy\rangle, then dz = \nabla f(a,b)\cdot \mathbf{u} where \nabla f = \langle f_x,\;f_y\rangle is the gradient vector.

It is important to note that a function f(x,y) may have partial derivatives without being fully differentiable (meaning the linear approximation might not perfectly describe small changes from that point). However, if f_x and f_y exist and are continuous in a neighborhood of (a,b), then f is differentiable at (a,b), and the total derivative exists. In such cases, the linear approximation is accurate for infinitesimal changes.

Example 4.1.

Use differentials to approximate the change in z for z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2} when (x,y) moves from (3,4) to (3.1, 4.2).

Solution: Here f(x,y) = \sqrt{x^2+y^2} (the distance from the origin). Compute partials:

f_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, \qquad f_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}

At (3,4), these evaluate to f_x(3,4)=\frac{3}{\sqrt{3^2+4^2}}=\frac{3}{5}} and f_y(3,4)=\frac{4}{\sqrt{3^2+4^2}}=\frac{4}{5}} (since \sqrt{3^2+4^2}=5). The differentials are dx=3.1-3=0.1, dy=4.2-4=0.2. So

dz \approx f_x(3,4)\,dx + f_y(3,4)\,dy = \frac{3}{5}(0.1) + \frac{4}{5}(0.2) = 0.03 + 0.16 = 0.19

We predict z will increase by about 0.19. The actual change: at (3.1,4.2), z=\sqrt{3.1^2+4.2^2}\approx 5.190, minus the original 5 gives \Delta z \approx 0.190, which agrees with the differential approximation.

In summary, the partial derivatives tell us the slopes in coordinate directions, while the total derivative (or differential) combines them to approximate changes in arbitrary directions. Partial derivatives hold one variable fixed,4 This is a footnote reiterating the concept of holding variables fixed for partial derivatives. whereas the total derivative allows all variables to vary.

Class 5: Differentiation of Composite Functions (Chain Rule)

Just as in single-variable calculus, we often deal with composite functions where one variable depends on another. In multivariable calculus, the Chain Rule generalizes to handle how changes propagate through nested dependencies.

Suppose z = f(x,y) where x and y themselves are functions of another variable t (say x=g(t) and y=h(t)). Then z is ultimately a function of t, and we can compute \frac{dz}{dt} by the Chain Rule:

\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y)\,\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x,y)\,\frac{dy}{dt} = f_x(x,y)\,\frac{dx}{dt} + f_y(x,y)\,\frac{dy}{dt}

This follows because dz = f_x\,dx + f_y\,dy and \frac{dx}{dt}, \frac{dy}{dt} describe how x,y change with t. So we accumulate the changes: f_x times \frac{dx}{dt} plus f_y times \frac{dy}{dt}.5 This is a footnote explaining the accumulation of changes in the chain rule.

More generally, if z=f(u,v) is a function of intermediate variables u,v which in turn depend on other variables (say u = u(x,y) and v = v(x,y)), we apply the Chain Rule in partial derivative form. For example, if z=f(u,v) with u=g(x,y) and v=h(x,y), then

\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial u}(u,v)\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(x,y) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(u,v)\,\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}(x,y) = f_u\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + f_v\,\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}

and similarly

\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial u}(u,v)\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}(x,y) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}(u,v)\,\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}(x,y) = f_u\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} + f_v\,\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}

Here f_u denotes the partial derivative of f with respect to its first argument u, and f_v is the partial with respect to v. Essentially, we multiply along each path of dependence and sum up the contributions.6 This is a footnote explaining the path dependence and summation in the multivariate chain rule. This can be visualized with a dependency tree diagram: z depends on u and v, which each depend on x and y. Every path from x to z and from y to z yields a term in the derivative.

For instance, consider z(p,q) = p^2q as a function of p,q, and p = x+y, q = x-y. Then z = f(x,y) = (x+y)^2 (x-y). We can find f_x using the chain rule:

\frac{\partial z}{\partial p} = 2pq, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial q} = p^2

and \frac{\partial p}{\partial x} = 1, \frac{\partial q}{\partial x} = 1. So

f_x = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial p} \cdot \frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial q} \cdot \frac{\partial q}{\partial x} = 2pq \cdot 1 + p^2 \cdot 1 = 2pq + p^2

Substituting back p=x+y, q=x-y gives

f_x = 2(x+y)(x-y) + (x+y)^2

Alternatively, one could expand z=(x+y)^2(x-y) directly and differentiate, but the chain rule provides a systematic approach without full expansion.

The Chain Rule can extend to multiple layers of composition and more variables. If w = F(x,y,z) and each of x,y,z are functions of parameters, the structure is similar: differentiate F with respect to each input and multiply by the derivative of that input with respect to your parameter.

Example 6.1.

Let z = e^{u} \sin v, where u = x^2 + y and v = x y. Find \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} and \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}.

Solution: We have f(u,v) = e^u \sin v. The partial derivatives of f are f_u = e^u \sin v and f_v = e^u \cos v. Now u(x,y)=x^2+y and v(x,y)=xy. Compute u- and v-partials with respect to x and y:

\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 2x, \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = 1, \qquad \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = y, \quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = x

Now apply the chain rule:

\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial u}\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = e^u \sin v \cdot (2x) + e^u \cos v \cdot (y) = e^u (2x\sin v + y\cos v)
\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial u}\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = e^u \sin v \cdot (1) + e^u \cos v \cdot (x) = e^u (\sin v + x\cos v)

Finally, substitute back u and v:

\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = e^{(x^2+y)}\big(2x\sin(xy) + y\cos(xy)\big), \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = e^{(x^2+y)}\big(\sin(xy) + x\cos(xy)\big)

The Chain Rule is a powerful tool that allows us to handle complex dependency relationships systematically.7 This is a footnote highlighting the power and systematic nature of the chain rule. It's especially useful in engineering contexts, where one quantity often depends on others that themselves depend on different variables (for example, temperature depending on time and position, position depending on time, etc.).

Class 6: Differentiation Using Polar Coordinates

Sometimes it is convenient to use a different coordinate system to simplify differentiation. In the plane, polar coordinates (r,\theta) relate to Cartesian (x,y) via

x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta

Here r \ge 0 is the radial distance from the origin, and \theta is the angle from the positive x-axis.8 This is a footnote defining polar coordinates r and \theta. We can express a surface z=f(x,y) as z = f(r\cos\theta,\;r\sin\theta), effectively viewing z as a function of r and \theta: say g(r,\theta) = f(r\cos\theta,\;r\sin\theta).

Differentiating such a function can be done by the chain rule. For example, to find \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} in terms of r,\theta, treat x and y as functions of r,\theta:

\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\frac{\partial r}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial \theta}\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}

From x=r\cos\theta and y=r\sin\theta, one can derive:

r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan\frac{y}{x}

It can be shown that

\frac{\partial r}{\partial x} = \cos\theta, \qquad \frac{\partial r}{\partial y} = \sin\theta
\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = -\frac{\sin\theta}{r}, \qquad \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} = \frac{\cos\theta}{r}

Thus, using f(r,\theta) = g(r,\theta) notation for convenience:

f_x = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \cdot \frac{\partial r}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = f_r \cdot \cos\theta + f_\theta \cdot \Big(-\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\Big)
f_y = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \cdot \frac{\partial r}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} = f_r \cdot \sin\theta + f_\theta \cdot \Big(\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\Big)

(Here f_r = \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} and f_\theta=\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}.) These are the formulas for converting partial derivatives from polar to Cartesian coordinates.

Alternatively, one can compute partials in polar coordinates by first converting the function: If z = f(x,y) = f(r\cos\theta, \; r\sin\theta), then one may find f_r and f_\theta directly and then use the above relations.

Figure 3: Polar coordinate system: a point is described by (r,\theta) giving its distance from the origin and angle from the horizontal. Switching to polar coordinates can simplify differentiation when f(x,y) has circular symmetry or depends naturally on r and \theta.

Example 7.1.

Suppose z = x^2 + y^2 = r^2 (a function that depends only on r). Compute z_x and z_y directly and via polar derivative formulas.

Solution: Directly, z_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2+y^2) = 2x and z_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2+y^2) = 2y. In polar coordinates, note that f(r,\theta) = r^2 so f_r = \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2) = 2r and f_\theta=\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(r^2)=0 (since r^2 doesn't depend on \theta). Then

f_x = f_r\cos\theta + f_\theta(-\sin\theta/r) = 2r\cos\theta + 0 = 2r\cos\theta

But r\cos\theta = x, so f_x = 2x. Similarly f_y = 2r\sin\theta = 2y. This matches the direct result.

Often, if f is given in terms of r and \theta, using these formulas or the chain rule can be far simpler than converting back to x,y and then differentiating.

As another example, consider z = \sin\!\big(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\big). Here it is natural to set u = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}=r. Then z = \sin r. We have

\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{dz}{dr} \cdot \frac{\partial r}{\partial x} = \cos r \cdot \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}} = \cos(\sqrt{x^2+y^2})\cdot \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}

So z_x = \frac{x}{r}\cos r and similarly z_y = \frac{y}{r}\cos r. This can be obtained in one step by recognizing radial symmetry and differentiating with respect to r.

Polar (as well as cylindrical and spherical in 3D) coordinates are particularly useful when f has some symmetry or when converting to these coordinates simplifies the form of the function.

Class 7: Definition of Extrema

In single-variable calculus, a point x=a is a local maximum if f(a) is greater than or equal to f(x) for x near a. Similarly, x=a is a local minimum if f(a) is less than or equal to nearby values. For functions of two variables, the idea is analogous: we consider a point (a,b) and compare f(a,b) to values of f(x,y) for (x,y) in a neighborhood of (a,b).

A point (a,b) is called a local maximum of f(x,y) if f(a,b) \ge f(x,y) for all (x,y) sufficiently close to (a,b). Likewise, (a,b) is a local minimum if f(a,b) \le f(x,y) for all points near (a,b). Collectively, local maxima and minima are called local extrema (extreme values). If one of these inequalities is strict for nearby points (except at the point itself), we say it is a strict max or min. If the inequality holds globally (for all (x,y) in the domain), then (a,b) is a global (absolute) maximum or minimum.

Geometrically, a local maximum of z=f(x,y) is like a peak or hilltop on the surface, and a local minimum is like a valley or bowl. At a local extremum, the surface is flat in the sense that moving in any small direction does not increase z (for a max) or does not decrease z (for a min). Points that are not extrema can be regular points or saddle points. A saddle point is where f_x=0 and f_y=0 (so it's flat in tangent) but it is not a max or min -- the surface goes up in one direction and down in another (like a horse saddle shape).

To find extrema of f(x,y), one typically looks at critical points: points where the gradient \nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle = \mathbf{0} or where one of the partials does not exist. This is analogous to f'(x)=0 in one variable. It's a necessary condition for differentiable extrema that \nabla f = \mathbf{0} at interior points.9 This is a footnote stating the necessary condition for extrema using gradients. However, not every critical point is an extremum (it could be a saddle or a flat point).

Example 9.1.

Consider f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2. This is a paraboloid opening upward. The point (0,0) is clearly the lowest point of this surface. Indeed, f(0,0)=0, and f(x,y)=x^2+y^2 > 0 for any (x,y)\neq(0,0). So (0,0) is a global (and local) minimum. There are no other critical points since \nabla f = \langle 2x, 2y \rangle is zero only at (0,0). No maximum exists since f can be made arbitrarily large as (x,y) goes far from the origin.

Example 9.2.

Let g(x,y) = -x^2 - y^2. This is the upside-down bowl (an inverted paraboloid). By a similar argument, (0,0) is a global maximum of g with value 0, and no minimum exists (the function goes to -\infty as (x,y) grows).

Example 9.3.

Let h(x,y) = x^2 - y^2. This surface is a saddle (hyperbolic paraboloid). The critical point (0,0) yields h(0,0)=0. If you move along the x-axis (y=0), h(x,0)=x^2\ge0, so (0,0) looks like a minimum in that cross-section. But if you move along the y-axis (x=0), h(0,y)=-y^2\le 0, so (0,0) looks like a maximum along that slice. In fact, (0,0) is neither a max nor a min -- it's a saddle point. Near (0,0), h takes both positive and negative values. This shows that the condition h_x=0,\,h_y=0 (which holds at (0,0)) is not sufficient to guarantee an extremum. We need further tests to classify the critical point.

Figure 4: Saddle point on the surface z=x^2 - y^2. Along one direction the surface curves upward (like a valley) and along an orthogonal direction it curves downward (like a ridge). Such a point is a critical point but not an extremum.

From these examples, we see that identifying a critical point is only the first step. We need a method to determine whether a given critical point is a local max, local min, or neither. This is where the second derivative (or Hessian) test comes in, which we will discuss soon.

Class 8: Extrema of Quadratic Functions

A common and important class of functions are quadratic forms or quadratic functions of two variables. These are functions of the form:

f(x,y) = Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F

where A, B, C, D, E, F are constants (with A,C not both zero). The graph of z=f(x,y) is a quadratic surface (also called a quadric). Examples include ellipsoids, paraboloids, hyperboloids, etc., depending on the coefficients. The shape of the surface and the nature of its extrema can be determined by analyzing the quadratic part Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2.

The critical point(s) of a quadratic function can be found by setting the first partial derivatives to zero:

f_x(x,y) = 2Ax + By + D = 0, \qquad f_y(x,y) = Bx + 2Cy + E = 0

This is a linear system for x and y. Solving it (if possible) gives the coordinates of the critical point (there will be exactly one critical point if the 2\times2 coefficient matrix \begin{pmatrix}2A & B\\ B & 2C\end{pmatrix} is invertible, i.e., if 4AC - B^2 \neq 0).

For example, consider f(x,y) = 3x^2 + 4xy + y^2 - 8x + 2. We find:

f_x = 6x + 4y - 8, \qquad f_y = 4x + 2y

Setting f_x=0 and f_y=0 leads to the linear system:

6x + 4y = 8, \qquad 4x + 2y = 0

Solving, from the second equation 4x = -2y \implies x = -\frac{y}{2}. Substitute into the first: 6(-\frac{y}{2}) + 4y = 8, i.e., -3y + 4y = 8, so y=8. Then x=-4. Thus (x,y)=(-4,8) is the only critical point. To classify it, we would check second derivatives (below).

The quadratic part Q(x,y)=Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 can be analyzed by completing the square or by linear algebra (eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix). The sign of Q(x,y) tells whether the surface opens upward (like a bowl), downward (like an upside bowl), or saddle-shaped. In particular, for the quadratic form Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2:

  • If A>0 and 4AC - B^2>0 (which implies C>0 as well), then Q(x,y) is positive definite (always >0 except at the origin). The surface z=Q(x,y) is shaped like an elliptic paraboloid opening upward, so the critical point is a local minimum.
  • If A<0 and 4AC - B^2>0, (C<0 too) then Q(x,y) is negative definite (always <0 except at origin). The surface opens downward and the critical point is a local maximum.
  • If 4AC - B^2 < 0, then Q is indefinite (takes both positive and negative values) and the surface z=Q(x,y) is a saddle. The critical point is a saddle point.
  • If 4AC - B^2 = 0, the test is inconclusive; the quadratic might be a parabolic cylinder or have a flat direction, and we need further analysis.

These conditions come from the second derivative test, which we formally introduce in the next section.10 This is a footnote connecting the quadratic function analysis to the second derivative test. For quadratic functions, the test is exact because the function's behavior is entirely determined by the quadratic part (higher-order terms are zero).

Figure 5: Elliptic paraboloid z=x^2+y^2 (minimum at the origin). Quadratic surfaces can be classified by the signs of their second-degree terms.

As a concrete example, the function f(x,y) = 3x^2 + 4xy + y^2 - 8x + 2 we solved above has A=3, B=4, C=1. Compute D = 4AC - B^2 = 4(3)(1) - 4^2 = 12 - 16 = -4. Since D<0, the quadratic part is indefinite, so the critical point (-4,8) is a saddle point. Indeed, if we examine the Hessian matrix

H = \begin{pmatrix}6 & 4\\4 & 2\end{pmatrix}

it has eigenvalues of opposite signs (one positive, one negative), confirming a saddle point.

Quadratic functions are a good testbed for understanding extrema: they are simple enough to solve exactly and illustrate the possibilities of min, max, or saddle. The criteria we used will generalize.

Class 9: Taylor Expansion and Extrema

To extend the intuition from quadratic functions to general f(x,y), we can use a Taylor expansion around a critical point. Suppose f is a C^2 (twice continuously differentiable) function and (a,b) is a critical point, so f_x(a,b)=0 and f_y(a,b)=0. The second-order Taylor polynomial of f around (a,b) is:

f(a+h,b+k) \approx f(a,b) + \frac{1}{2}\Big[f_{xx}(a,b)\,h^2 + 2f_{xy}(a,b)\,hk + f_{yy}(a,b)\,k^2\Big]

The linear (first-order) terms vanish because f_x(a,b)=f_y(a,b)=0. So near (a,b), the behavior of f is dominated by the quadratic form

Q(h,k) = \frac{1}{2}\big[f_{xx}(a,b)h^2 + 2f_{xy}(a,b)hk + f_{yy}(a,b)k^2\big]

(We can omit the \frac12 for classification purposes and focus on f_{xx}(a,b)h^2 + 2f_{xy}(a,b)hk + f_{yy}(a,b)k^2.)

Now, if Q(h,k) is positive for all small (h,k)\neq(0,0), then f(a+h,b+k) \approx f(a,b) + (\text{positive}), meaning f(a+h,b+k) > f(a,b) for nearby points. In that case, (a,b) is a local minimum. If Q(h,k) is negative for all small (h,k)\neq(0,0), then f(a+h,b+k) < f(a,b) near the point, giving a local maximum. If Q(h,k) takes both positive and negative values in different directions, then (a,b) is a saddle point.

Therefore, classifying a critical point largely boils down to analyzing the sign of the quadratic form given by the Hessian matrix:

H(a,b) = \begin{pmatrix} f_{xx}(a,b) & f_{xy}(a,b) \\ f_{xy}(a,b) & f_{yy}(a,b) \end{pmatrix}

This symmetric matrix encapsulates the second-order behavior of f at (a,b).

For convenience, define

D = D(a,b) = f_{xx}(a,b)\,f_{yy}(a,b) - [f_{xy}(a,b)]^2

which is the determinant of the Hessian matrix (often denoted D(a,b)). Also let A = f_{xx}(a,b). Then:

  • If D > 0 and A = f_{xx}(a,b) > 0, Q(h,k) is positive definite (both eigenvalues of H positive), so (a,b) is a local minimum.
  • If D > 0 and A = f_{xx}(a,b) < 0, Q(h,k) is negative definite (both eigenvalues negative), so (a,b) is a local maximum.
  • If D < 0, the eigenvalues of H have opposite signs, so Q(h,k) takes both positive and negative values. Then (a,b) is a saddle point.
  • If D = 0, the test is inconclusive; higher-order terms might determine the behavior.

These conclusions are exactly what we observed for quadratic functions, now generalized by the Taylor expansion reasoning.11 This is a footnote connecting the second derivative test to Taylor expansion. Essentially, near a non-degenerate critical point, a surface looks like a squished paraboloid (either bowl-shaped, dome-shaped, or saddle-shaped), determined by the second-order part of its Taylor series.

Example 10.1.

Let f(x,y) = x^2 + 2xy + 2y^2 + \cdots (higher order terms omitted). The quadratic part is x^2 + 2xy + 2y^2. Write it in matrix form to identify the Hessian's determinant: D = (1)(2) - (1)^2 = 2-1 = 1>0 and f_{xx}=2>0. So we expect a local min at (0,0). If the full f is given, we would confirm (0,0) is critical and then these second derivatives indeed predict a minimum.

Example 10.2.

If g(x,y) = -3x^2 + 4xy - 2y^2, at (0,0) the quadratic part is -3x^2 + 4xy - 2y^2. Compute D = (-3)(-2) - (2)^2 = 6 - 4 = 2>0 and g_{xx}=-6<0, so we predict a local maximum at (0,0).

In practice, one does not usually explicitly write the Taylor polynomial; one directly uses the second derivative test criteria above to classify critical points.

Class 10: Criteria for Extrema

We now summarize the second derivative test for functions of two variables:

Suppose f(x,y) has continuous second partial derivatives. Let (a,b) be a critical point of f (meaning f_x(a,b)=0 and f_y(a,b)=0). Compute

D = D(a,b) = f_{xx}(a,b)\,f_{yy}(a,b) - [f_{xy}(a,b)]^2

Then:

Condition Conclusion Hessian
D > 0 and f_{xx}(a,b) > 0 f(a,b) is a local minimum.12 Condition for local minimum using second derivative test. Positive definite
D > 0 and f_{xx}(a,b) < 0 f(a,b) is a local maximum.13 Condition for local maximum using second derivative test. Negative definite
D < 0 (a,b) is a saddle point: f has no extremum there.14 Condition for saddle point using second derivative test. Indefinite
D = 0 The test is inconclusive. The point could be any of a min, max, saddle, or even a flat higher-order critical point. One would need to investigate further or use higher derivatives. -

These criteria align with our earlier discussion. The quantity D is essentially the discriminant of the quadratic form (sometimes denoted AC - B^2 or similar in other contexts). The condition D>0 ensures both eigenvalues of the Hessian have the same sign; f_{xx}(a,b)'s sign then tells which sign that is (positive for min, negative for max). D<0 means one eigenvalue is positive and the other negative, giving a saddle.

Example 11.1.

f(x,y) = x^3 + y^3 - 3xy + 5. Find and classify critical points.

First, f_x = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 3x^2 - 3y, f_y = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3y^2 - 3x. Setting these equal to 0:

3x^2 - 3y = 0 \implies y = x^2, \qquad 3y^2 - 3x = 0 \implies x = y^2

Substitute y=x^2 into x=y^2: x = (x^2)^2 = x^4. So either x=0 or x^4 - x = x(x^3-1) = 0 \implies x^3=1 \implies x=1. Thus x=0 or x=1. If x=0, then from y=x^2, y=0. If x=1, then y=1^2=1 (and check x=y^2 gives 1=1, consistent). So two critical points: (0,0) and (1,1).

Now the second derivatives:

f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} = 6x, \qquad f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = 6y, \qquad f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\,\partial x} = -3

At (0,0): D = D(0,0) = (6\cdot0)(6\cdot0) - (-3)^2 = -9 < 0. Saddle point at (0,0).
At (1,1): D = D(1,1) = (6)(6) - (-3)^2 = 36 - 9 = 27 > 0, and f_{xx}(1,1)=6>0. So (1,1) is a local minimum.

Example 11.2.

g(x,y) = 4x^2 + y^4 - 4x + 1. Find and classify critical points.

Compute g_x = \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} = 8x - 4, g_y = \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} = 4y^3. Setting 0: 8x-4=0 \implies x=1/2; 4y^3=0 \implies y=0. So (\frac12,\,0) is the only critical point. Second derivatives: g_{xx}=\frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial x^2}=8, g_{yy}=\frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial y^2}=12y^2, g_{xy}=\frac{\partial^2 g}{\partial y\,\partial x}=0. At (1/2,0): D = D(\frac12,0) = 8 \cdot 12\cdot0^2 - 0^2 = 0. The test is inconclusive. We inspect g(1/2,0) and the function's behavior: g(1/2,0) = 4(1/4) + 0 - 4(1/2) + 1 = 1 - 2 + 1 = 0. If we move in the y-direction with x=1/2, g(1/2,y) = 4(1/4) + y^4 - 2 + 1 = y^4 = \ge 0, and equals 0 only at y=0. If we move in x-direction with y=0, g(x,0) = 4x^2 - 4x + 1 = 4(x-\tfrac12)^2 + 0, which is \ge 0 and equals 0 only at x=1/2. In any other direction, both x and y change and the dominant term for small moves might be y^4 or 4(x-1/2)^2. It appears g(x,y)\ge 0 for all x,y and (1/2,0) is actually a global minimum. The Hessian test failed because g_{yy}=0 at the point (the surface is very flat in the y-direction up to third order). This example shows why D=0 is inconclusive; one needs to examine higher-order terms or other reasoning.

In summary, to find extrema:

  1. Find all critical points (solve f_x=0, f_y=0).
  2. Apply the second derivative test to classify each critical point as local min, local max, or saddle (or inconclusive).
  3. Check boundaries of domain if seeking global extrema (if domain is closed/bounded, check also boundary points).
Class 11: Differentiation of Mappings and the Jacobian

Up to now, we focused on scalar-valued functions (from \mathbb{R}^n to \mathbb{R}). We now consider vector-valued functions (mappings) F: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m. For example, a planar change of coordinates (u,v) = F(x,y). The derivative of such a mapping is encapsulated by the Jacobian matrix, which is the matrix of all first-order partial derivatives.15 This is a footnote defining the Jacobian matrix as the matrix of first-order partial derivatives.

If F(x,y) = (u(x,y),\,v(x,y)), then the Jacobian matrix is:

J_F(x,y) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \\ \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} u_x & u_y \\ v_x & v_y \end{pmatrix}

This 2\times 2 matrix linearly approximates F near a point. More precisely, if (x,y) changes by (dx,dy), then (du,dv) \approx (u_x\,dx + u_y\,dy,\; v_x\,dx + v_y\,dy), analogous to the total derivative for scalars. In higher dimensions, say F:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^2 with F(x,y,z)=(u,v), the Jacobian would be 2\times 3:

J_F(x,y,z) = \begin{pmatrix} u_x & u_y & u_z \\ v_x & v_y & v_z \end{pmatrix}

Each row is the gradient of one component function, and each column represents the partial derivatives with respect to one input variable.

The Jacobian matrix generalizes the concept of a derivative to multivariate vector functions.16 This is a footnote emphasizing the generalization aspect of the Jacobian matrix. If m=1 (one output), the Jacobian matrix is a 1-row matrix – essentially the gradient vector. If n=1 (one input, like a parametric curve), the Jacobian matrix is a column of first derivatives (which you might call the velocity vector in that case). So it includes these as special cases.

One important scalar derived from the Jacobian matrix is the Jacobian determinant. For F:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2, \det(J_F) describes how areas scale under the mapping F. For example, in polar coordinates F(r,\theta)=(x,y)=(r\cos\theta,\;r\sin\theta), the Jacobian matrix is J_F(r,\theta) = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}. The determinant is \det(J_F) = r\cos^2\theta - (-r\sin^2\theta) = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r. This tells us, for instance, that a small area dr\,d\theta in r\theta-space is stretched to area r\,dr\,d\theta in xy-space. This factor r is exactly what appears when converting integrals to polar coordinates.

Another example: consider the mapping T(x,y)=(u,v)=(2x+y,\;x-3y). Its Jacobian matrix is J_T(x,y) = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\ 1 & -3\end{pmatrix} with determinant \det(J_T) = (2)(-3) - (1)(1) = -6 - 1 = -7. A small shape in the xy-plane will be distorted by T into a shape in the uv-plane with area approximately |-7| = 7 times larger (the sign indicates orientation flip but magnitude 7 is the area scale).

When F:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n is invertible near a point, the Jacobian determinant being nonzero is essential. In fact, the Inverse Function Theorem states that if \det J_F(a) \neq 0, then F is locally invertible around a, and the Jacobian matrix of the inverse function is the matrix inverse of J_F.17 This is a footnote introducing the Inverse Function Theorem and its relation to the Jacobian determinant. We will not dive deeply into the theory, but this idea is quite powerful.

In summary, to differentiate a mapping F(x,y,\dots), differentiate each component. The collection of partial derivatives forms the Jacobian matrix. This matrix can be used to transform differentials: (du,dv) = J_F(x,y)\cdot(dx,dy) in matrix form. It is also used in change-of-variable for integrals, stability analysis in differential equations (Jacobian eigenvalues), and more.

Class 12: Inverse Functions

The concept of an inverse function generalizes to multiple dimensions. If y = f(x) (single variable) is invertible, we know x = f^{-1}(y) and \frac{dx}{dy} = \frac{1}{f'(x)} at corresponding points, assuming f'(x)\neq0. In higher dimensions, an invertible mapping F:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n locally has an inverse if its Jacobian determinant is nonzero at a point (this ensures the linear approximation is invertible).

More formally, the Inverse Function Theorem states: If F(\mathbf{a})=\mathbf{b} and the Jacobian matrix J_F(\mathbf{a}) is invertible (nonzero determinant), then there exists a neighborhood of \mathbf{a} in which F has a differentiable inverse F^{-1}. Moreover, (J_{F^{-1}}(\mathbf{b})) = (J_F(\mathbf{a}))^{-1}.18 This is a footnote stating the formal relationship between Jacobians of inverse functions. In other words, to get the derivative of the inverse mapping at \mathbf{b}, invert the matrix of the derivative of F at \mathbf{a}.

For a simple 2D illustration, consider u = 2x + y,\; v = x + 3y. This is a linear mapping F(x,y)=(u,v). The Jacobian is J_F(x,y) = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\1 & 3\end{pmatrix} with \det(J_F)=5. The mapping is invertible. We can find the inverse mapping explicitly by solving:

\begin{cases} 2x+y = u,\\ x+3y = v, \end{cases}

which gives (solving the linear system) x = \frac{3u - v}{5}, y = \frac{-u + 2v}{5} (using Cramer's rule or matrix inverse). The Jacobian matrix of the inverse F^{-1}(u,v)=(x(u,v),\;y(u,v)) is

J_{F^{-1}}(u,v) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3/5 & -1/5 \\ -1/5 & 2/5 \end{pmatrix}

This is exactly the inverse of the original Jacobian matrix J_F^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\1 & 3\end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix}3 & -1\\-1 & 2\end{pmatrix}. This linear example reflects the general rule.

From a practical perspective, if you have functions u(x,y) and v(x,y) and can solve them for x and y in terms of u and v, you can differentiate implicitly to find the partial derivatives of the inverse. Usually, one sets up linear equations for \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}, etc., or uses matrix inversion of the Jacobian.

For single-variable invertible functions, this theorem reduces to: if \frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x) \neq 0, then \frac{dx}{dy} = \frac{1}{f'(x)}. For instance, if y=\ln x, then \frac{dy}{dx} = 1/x, so \frac{dx}{dy} = x = e^y, which is the derivative of the inverse function x = e^y.

One caution: if the Jacobian determinant is zero, the function may not be invertible (think of F(x,y)=(x^2,y) at (0,y), Jacobian det=0 leads to not one-to-one around that point).

Class 13: Implicit Functions

Often we have relationships given implicitly, like F(x,y)=0, defining y as a function of x indirectly. The Implicit Function Theorem provides conditions under which we can solve for one variable as a differentiable function of the other variables locally. In essence: if F(x,y)=0 and at a point (a,b) on this level set we have \frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(a,b)\neq 0, then in a neighborhood of that point we can solve for y as a function of x, say y = g(x), and g'(x) = -\frac{F_x}{F_y} at that point.19 This is a footnote introducing the Implicit Function Theorem and the formula for implicit differentiation.

This result is basically an application of the chain rule and inverse function theorem. Differentiating F(x,y(x)) \equiv 0 with respect to x:

\frac{d}{dx}F(x,y(x)) = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \frac{dx}{dx} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \frac{dy}{dx} = F_x + F_y \frac{dy}{dx} = 0

so

\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}

assuming F_y \neq 0.20 This is a footnote showing the derivation of the implicit differentiation formula. This is the familiar formula from implicit differentiation in calculus. It says the slope of the implicit curve F(x,y)=0 is given by the negative ratio of the partial derivatives.

For example, the circle F(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 - R^2 = 0 implicitly defines y as a function of x (except at vertical tangents). We have F_x = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = 2x, F_y = \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} = 2y. The formula gives \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y} = -\frac{2x}{2y} = -\frac{x}{y}. So the slope of the circle at (x,y) is -x/y (which makes sense: it's the negative reciprocal of y/x, the slope of the radius). This matches classical geometry of a circle.

As another example, suppose we have F(x,y,z) = xyz^2 + \sin x + e^y - 5 = 0 defining z implicitly as a function of x and y near a solution. To find \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}, we treat y as independent and z=z(x,y) as dependent. Differentiate implicitly with respect to x:

\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xyz^2 + \sin x + e^y - 5) = 0

Note e^y is constant with respect to x. We get

\left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy) \cdot z^2 + xy \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(z^2) \right) + \cos x + 0 - 0 = 0
\left( y \cdot z^2 + xy \cdot 2z \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} \right) + \cos x = 0
yz^2 + 2xyz \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} + \cos x = 0

Now solve for \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}:

2xyz\,\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -(yz^2 + \cos x) \implies \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{yz^2 + \cos x}{2xyz}

We needed F_z = \frac{\partial F}{\partial z} = 2xyz (the partial of xyz^2 with respect to z) to be nonzero to isolate \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}. The formula \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z} indeed gives the same result here if applied carefully.

In general, for F(x,y,z)=0 defining z as a function of x,y,

\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{F_y}{F_z}

provided F_z \neq 0. These are the partial derivatives of the implicit function z(x,y). One must ensure the condition (F_z\neq0) at the point, otherwise we can't solve for z uniquely (vertical tangent in higher dimensions).

The Implicit Function Theorem assures that if F(x_0,y_0,z_0)=0 and F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)\neq0, there exists a local function z=f(x,y) passing through that point such that F(x,y,f(x,y))=0. The derivatives we computed are part of that theorem's conclusion.

Example 13.1.

Find \frac{dy}{dx} for the implicit equation x^2 e^y + y = 1.

Differentiate both sides with respect to x:

\frac{d}{dx}(x^2 e^y + y) = \frac{d}{dx}(1)
\frac{d}{dx}(x^2 e^y) + \frac{dy}{dx} = 0
\left( \frac{d}{dx}(x^2) \cdot e^y + x^2 \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(e^y) \right) + \frac{dy}{dx} = 0
\left( 2x e^y + x^2 e^y \frac{dy}{dx} \right) + \frac{dy}{dx} = 0

Here F(x,y)=x^2e^y + y - 1. So F_x = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = 2xe^y, F_y = \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} = x^2 e^y + 1. Solve for \frac{dy}{dx}:

\frac{dy}{dx}(x^2 e^y + 1) = -2x e^y

so

\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{2x e^y}{x^2 e^y + 1}

That is the slope of the curve defined implicitly, as long as x^2 e^y + 1 \neq 0 (which it never is here, so it's fine).

Implicit differentiation becomes especially handy for complicated equations where solving for one variable explicitly is difficult. It also sets the stage for understanding constrained extrema (using methods like Lagrange multipliers, where one handles constraints implicitly).

  • Focusing on quadratic functions, we learned how to find their critical points and classify them by examining the quadratic form. This led to understanding how the second partial derivatives determine the nature of a critical point.
  • Through Taylor expansion around a critical point, we justified the second derivative test. We provided criteria involving the Hessian determinant D and the second partials to classify a critical point as a local min, local max, or saddle.25 Summary point about the second derivative test for extrema.
  • We discussed Jacobian matrices as a way to collect partial derivatives of vector-valued functions.26 Summary point about Jacobian matrices. The Jacobian gives a linear approximation of a multivariable mapping and its determinant indicates local scaling (particularly important in coordinate transformations and the inverse function theorem).
  • The Inverse Function Theorem was stated in essence: a non-zero Jacobian determinant ensures a local inverse exists and how to get its derivative.27 Summary point about the Inverse Function Theorem.
  • We covered implicit differentiation and the Implicit Function Theorem, showing how to find derivatives of a function defined implicitly by an equation (like F(x,y)=0) by using \frac{dy}{dx} = -F_x/F_y.28 Summary point about implicit differentiation and the Implicit Function Theorem.

Throughout, our focus has been on developing intuition and tools: partial derivatives generalize single-variable differentiation, gradients and Jacobians generalize slopes, and the interplay of these tools lets us analyze how multivariable functions change and how to optimize them. With this foundation, one can confidently tackle a variety of engineering and physics problems involving multivariate functions, whether it's finding the optimal design parameters (extrema problems) or analyzing how changes in inputs propagate to changes in outputs (sensitivity via derivatives).
